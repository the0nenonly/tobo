20 tips to stay sustainable outside

01

Turn off your engine at red lights

Idling over 10 seconds burns more fuel than restarting. Modern engines are built for frequent restarts with no added wear.

10 minutes of idling burns ~0.26 litres of fuel and emits 600g of CO₂ for zero km travelled.
The US EPA estimates idling wastes 6 billion gallons of fuel per year nationally.
02

Walk or cycle trips under 2 km

Short car trips are the most polluting per kilometre — the engine hasn't warmed up, so combustion is inefficient.

Cars emit up to 60% more CO₂ per km in the first 5 minutes of a cold engine start.
Cycling produces 21g CO₂/km vs 271g CO₂/km for an average petrol car (Our World in Data).
03

Bring a reusable bag

Single-use plastic bags contribute massively to landfill and ocean pollution. A cotton tote used regularly eliminates hundreds of bags a year.

The average person uses 307 plastic bags per year; a reusable bag needs just 11 uses to break even.
Over 8 million tonnes of plastic enter the world's oceans each year — bags are among the top contributors.
04

Use public transport when possible

A full bus emits 4–9× less CO₂ per passenger than a single-occupancy car. Even occasional use makes a measurable annual difference.

Switching one car commute per week to public transport saves ~1.5 tonnes CO₂ per person annually.
Light rail produces 62g CO₂/passenger-km vs 192g for a single-occupancy car (European Environment Agency).
05

Carry a reusable water bottle

Disposable plastic bottles require oil to produce and rarely get recycled. A reusable bottle eliminates hundreds of single-use plastics every year.

It takes 3 litres of water to produce just 1 litre of bottled water.
Only 30% of plastic bottles in the US are recycled; the rest go to landfill or become pollution.
06

Carpool with colleagues or neighbours

Sharing a journey with one other person halves each person's per-km emissions. It also reduces road congestion.

If every US commuter carpooled just 1 day per week, it would save 37 million gallons of fuel weekly.
Average car occupancy in the US is just 1.5 people — close to the worst possible efficiency.
07

Keep your car tyres properly inflated

Under-inflated tyres increase rolling resistance, forcing the engine to work harder and burn more fuel on every journey.

Tyres inflated 10 PSI below recommended reduce fuel efficiency by about 3.3% (US DOE).
Correct tyre pressure across the US fleet could save 1.2 billion gallons of fuel annually.
08

Buy local produce when shopping

Food transported shorter distances requires less fuel for refrigeration and shipping. Local markets also tend to use less packaging.

"Food miles" account for about 6% of all food system greenhouse gas emissions globally (Nature Food, 2022).
The average meal in the US travels over 1,500 miles from farm to plate.
09

Avoid fast fashion — choose quality

Clothing production is one of the world's most polluting industries. Buying fewer, better-made garments dramatically cuts your fashion footprint.

The fashion industry produces 10% of global carbon emissions — more than aviation and shipping combined.
85% of all textiles end up in landfills each year; the equivalent of one rubbish truck per second.
10

Don't litter — dispose of waste responsibly

Litter that reaches waterways contributes to microplastic pollution. Always use bins or take rubbish home when one isn't available.

Microplastics have now been found in human blood, lung tissue, and even placenta.
Cigarette butts are the world's most littered item — and are not biodegradable.
11

Choose plant-based meals when eating out

Meat, especially beef, requires large amounts of land, water, and energy. Opting for plant-based dishes even occasionally makes a real difference.

Producing 1 kg of beef emits up to 60 kg of CO₂-equivalent — 50× more than 1 kg of vegetables.
A plant-based diet can reduce food carbon footprint by up to 73% (University of Oxford).
12

Recycle correctly when outside

Contaminated recycling bins are often sent to landfill. Learning which items belong where saves entire batches from being rejected.

Recycling one aluminium can saves enough energy to run a TV for 3 hours.
25% of recycling in some cities is rejected due to contamination.
13

Avoid short-haul flights — take the train

Trains emit a fraction of the CO₂ of flights. For trips under 500 km, rail is almost always the lower-carbon option.

A return flight London to New York produces ~1.7 tonnes of CO₂ per passenger.
Rail emits 6–14g CO₂/km vs 255g/km for short-haul flights per passenger.
14

Refill, don't replace

Many products — cleaning sprays, shampoos, coffee — now offer refill options that use up to 90% less plastic than buying new.

Refillable containers reduce plastic waste per product by up to 90% vs single-use packaging.
The global refill market is projected to reach $73 billion by 2030.
15

Maintain a steady speed while driving

Aggressive acceleration and braking dramatically increases fuel consumption. Smooth driving can improve efficiency by up to 30%.

Driving at 110 km/h vs 90 km/h uses up to 25% more fuel (IEA).
Eco-driving courses reduce fuel use by 10–15% with no changes to routes or vehicles.
16

Support green businesses

Where you spend your money shapes what businesses do. Choosing shops and services with sustainability commitments encourages industry-wide change.

66% of consumers — and 73% of millennials — say they'd pay more for sustainable products (Nielsen).
Consumer spending drives 60–70% of global greenhouse gas emissions through supply chain demand.
17

Avoid single-use coffee cups

Most disposable coffee cups cannot be recycled due to their plastic lining. Bringing your own cup eliminates the waste entirely.

500 billion single-use cups are used globally every year — fewer than 1% are recycled.
Many cafés offer a 10–25 cent discount for reusable cups.
18

Plant a tree or support urban greening

Trees absorb CO₂, cool urban areas, and support biodiversity. Joining or donating to local tree-planting initiatives has lasting environmental benefits.

A single mature tree absorbs approximately 21 kg of CO₂ per year.
Urban trees reduce local temperatures by 2–8°C, cutting air conditioning demand.
19

Consolidate errands into one trip

Multiple short trips produce more total emissions than one combined journey due to cold-start inefficiency. Plan ahead to group outings.

Trip-chaining can reduce vehicle km travelled by 25%, directly cutting fuel consumption.
Cold engine starts produce 60–80× more pollution than a warm engine over the same distance.
20

Talk about it — share sustainable habits

Social influence is one of the most powerful drivers of behaviour change. Sharing what you do — without lecturing — can inspire people around you.

Social norms messaging is more effective than factual information alone at changing environmental behaviour.
Each person who adopts a sustainable habit influences 3–5 others in their social circle (Cambridge, 2019).
Sources: US EPA · European Environment Agency · Our World in Data · IEA · University of Oxford · Nature Food · Nielsen
All facts are for educational purposes. Individual impact varies by region and lifestyle.

20 tips to make your home more sustainable

01

Switch to LED light bulbs

LED bulbs use up to 90% less energy than traditional incandescent bulbs and last significantly longer, making them one of the easiest swaps you can make.

Replacing one 60W incandescent with an LED saves around 50 kg of CO₂ per year.
LEDs last up to 25 times longer than traditional bulbs — reducing waste and replacement costs.
02

Turn off appliances at the plug

Devices left on standby still draw power. Turning off TVs, chargers, and computers at the wall eliminates this invisible "phantom load."

Standby power accounts for 5–10% of residential electricity use in most countries (IEA).
The average UK household wastes £55 per year on standby power — cutting it costs nothing.
03

Wash laundry at 30°C

Most modern detergents are designed to work effectively at low temperatures. Washing at 30°C instead of 60°C dramatically cuts the energy used per cycle.

Washing at 30°C uses around 40% less energy than washing at 40°C.
If all UK households switched to 30°C washes, it would save over 500,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually.
04

Reduce shower time by 2 minutes

Hot water heating is one of the most energy-intensive parts of a home. Shaving just 2 minutes off your daily shower adds up over a year.

A 2-minute shorter shower saves around 10 litres of water and cuts water heating energy by ~12%.
Showers account for nearly 25% of all household water use in developed countries.
05

Lower your thermostat by 1°C

Heating is the biggest energy cost in most homes. A single degree reduction has a surprisingly large cumulative effect across a heating season.

Turning your thermostat down by 1°C can reduce your heating bill by up to 10% (Energy Saving Trust).
Space heating accounts for roughly 42% of all energy use in EU households.
06

Reduce food waste — plan your meals

Food waste is one of the most overlooked sources of household emissions. Planning meals and using leftovers prevents perfectly good food from going to landfill.

If food waste were a country, it would be the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases on the planet.
The average UK household throws away £700 worth of food each year.
07

Use a full dishwasher, not handwashing

A full, efficient dishwasher uses less water per item than handwashing. The key is running it only when full and using the eco setting.

A full dishwasher uses around 9–12 litres of water vs up to 60 litres for handwashing the same load.
Using the eco cycle cuts energy use by up to 45% compared to standard cycles.
08

Air-dry your laundry instead of tumble drying

Tumble dryers are among the most energy-hungry appliances in the home. Air-drying costs nothing and is gentler on fabrics too.

A tumble dryer uses around 4–5 kWh per cycle — more than any other household appliance per use.
Air-drying laundry can save the average household £150–£200 per year on energy bills.
09

Fix dripping taps immediately

A dripping tap might seem minor, but over a year the volume of wasted water is substantial. Fixing it is usually simple and cheap.

A tap dripping once per second wastes over 11,000 litres of water per year.
Household leaks in the US waste nearly 1 trillion gallons of water each year (EPA).
10

Compost food scraps

Food scraps sent to landfill produce methane — a greenhouse gas far more potent than CO₂. Home composting turns waste into a valuable resource for gardens.

Methane from landfills is 25× more potent as a greenhouse gas than CO₂ over 100 years.
Composting at home can divert up to 150 kg of food waste per household per year from landfill.
11

Insulate your home properly

Poor insulation means heat escapes and your boiler works harder. Draught-proofing windows and doors is one of the most cost-effective green home improvements.

Up to 25% of heat in an uninsulated home is lost through the roof (Energy Saving Trust).
Properly insulating a semi-detached home can save over 1 tonne of CO₂ per year.
12

Buy second-hand furniture and appliances

Manufacturing new furniture and appliances is extremely energy-intensive. Buying second-hand extends the life of existing goods and avoids new production entirely.

Buying a second-hand sofa instead of new saves the equivalent of around 90 kg of CO₂.
The resale furniture market is projected to grow to $16.6 billion by 2025 as demand surges.
13

Only boil what you need in the kettle

Overfilling the kettle is one of the most common and unnecessary energy wastes in the kitchen. Boil only the amount you actually need.

The UK wastes £68 million of electricity per year from overfilling kettles (Energy Saving Trust).
Boiling only the water you need can cut a kettle's energy use in half.
14

Switch to a green energy tariff

Many energy providers now offer tariffs backed by renewable sources. Switching is often free and can dramatically reduce your home's indirect emissions.

Switching to a 100% renewable electricity tariff can eliminate up to 1.5 tonnes of CO₂ from your annual footprint.
Electricity generation accounts for about 25% of global greenhouse gas emissions.
15

Use a low-flow showerhead

Low-flow showerheads deliver a satisfying shower while using significantly less water and energy — a one-time investment that pays back quickly.

A low-flow showerhead uses 6–8 litres/min vs 15–20 litres/min for a standard head — over 50% less water.
A family of four could save over 160,000 litres of water per year by switching.
16

Grow your own herbs or vegetables

Even a small windowsill herb garden reduces packaging waste and transport emissions. It also reconnects you with where food comes from.

Growing your own food eliminates transport, packaging, and refrigeration emissions associated with supermarket produce.
A 10 m² vegetable patch can produce up to 50 kg of food per year with minimal inputs.
17

Avoid single-use cleaning products

Many conventional cleaning sprays come in single-use plastic bottles. Concentrated tablets or refillable alternatives deliver the same result with far less waste.

Cleaning product packaging generates over 900 million plastic bottles of waste in the US every year.
Concentrated cleaning tablets use up to 98% less plastic packaging per clean than traditional spray bottles.
18

Use smart power strips

Smart strips automatically cut power to devices that aren't in use, eliminating standby drain from TVs, consoles, and entertainment setups without any effort.

Smart power strips can reduce home electricity use by 5–10% — saving £50–£100 per year in a typical household.
The average home has 40+ devices drawing standby power simultaneously.
19

Install a water-saving toilet flush

Older toilets can use up to 13 litres per flush. Dual-flush systems or cistern displacement devices reduce this significantly with no loss of function.

Toilets account for nearly 30% of indoor household water use (EPA).
A dual-flush toilet reduces water use per flush by up to 67% compared to older single-flush models.
20

Talk about it — inspire your household

Sustainability at home works best when everyone is on board. Involving family or housemates in the habits you build multiplies the impact without extra effort.

Households where all members adopt green habits reduce their footprint 2–3× more than individuals acting alone.
Social norms within a home are among the strongest predictors of sustainable energy behaviour (Nature Energy, 2021).
Sources: US EPA · Energy Saving Trust · IEA · European Environment Agency · Our World in Data · Nature Energy
